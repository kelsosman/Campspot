Thesis Slide

→ Slide 01 · "Stability Is the Strategy"

This slide is intentionally spare — the thesis is one sentence, supported by three sharp bullets. Don't over-explain it. Let the room sit with it.

If pushed on "what becomes commoditized": basic reservations, grid management, and OTA connectivity are now table stakes. Every major competitor offers them. The moat is the data layer — 3.7M reservations, 54M site nights, benchmarks operators can't get elsewhere — but only if operators trust the platform enough to stay and use it.

If pushed on "what becomes scarce": operator trust. No contracts means no switching friction beyond operational pain (data migration, staff retraining). A frustrated operator will absorb that pain. Word travels fast in ARVC and regional campground communities.

The Bet Slide

→ Slide 02a · "Earn Back the Right to Build"

The Shopify analogy is your strongest framing tool. Shopify's entire value proposition is "your store never goes down on Black Friday." For Campspot, that's Memorial Day, Fourth of July, and Labor Day. Shopify spent nine months and ran five major scale tests pushing infrastructure to 150% of expected load before peak season. Most companies load test once and hope. The discipline gap is the story.

On the CPO's AI-native codebase direction: honor it, sequence it. Suggested framing: "I'm excited about this direction. My recommendation is to pilot it on new modules while we stabilize the existing platform — not as an either/or."

Key questions to ask Engineering before committing to timeline: What are the uptime nines? Is there a CAB? What does the tech debt backlog actually look like — sprints or rebuild? Does an SRE function exist?

On the demo error: you logged into the Campspot demo in June 2026 and found an active error message on the reservation grid. That's firsthand, current-state evidence — not historical NPS data. Use it if challenged on whether the reliability issues are "old news."

Source note: AI code vulnerability stats (2.7x density, 75% tech debt, 67% debugging time) are from SQ Magazine aggregating "Second Talent" — the original primary source is not independently verifiable. Present as industry research directional, not hard data. If the CTO pushes, acknowledge the sourcing limitation.

Priorities Slide

→ Slide 02b · "What This Unlocks"

Primary priorities are bugs, not features. Billing issues that change past invoices, incorrect due dates, inability to correct payments — this is data integrity, not UX. Frame it to Engineering as such. "Billing integrity" lands differently than "invoicing complaints."

On dynamic pricing: current tool is rule-based. AI-powered suggestions launched November 14, 2025. The adoption gap (22% of operators vs. 67% of award-winning parks) signals the UX creates friction — the "all or nothing" setup feels risky to operators. The three-mode control model addresses this directly and is a product insight, not a feature request from operators. It's what they actually need, not what they've asked for — which is exactly the CPO's concern about the product trap.

On education: parks using Analytics make 61.8% more revenue. Parks using dynamic pricing make 70.3% more. The product is already there. The behavior isn't. That's an education and onboarding problem, not an engineering problem.

On SMS/email: connect it to the dynamic pricing engine explicitly. Dynamic pricing is only as powerful as the marketing that fills the gaps it creates. These are not separate features — they're one loop.

Not List Slide

→ Slide 03 · "What We're Not Doing"

The brief asks for at least one cut that customers are actively asking for. Drag-and-drop is the most defensible answer — operators asked for it loudly, it's been released, and the remaining gap is education, not engineering. If the demo error (active bug on the grid) comes up, the answer is: that's a stability issue, not a reason to rebuild.

On the full CRM: the more interesting question to raise is whether repeat visitation data justifies a lighter loyalty or CRM enhancement rather than a full build. KOA built their entire PMS (K2) specifically to own the loyalty relationship. That's the benchmark for why this matters — and why doing it badly would be worse than not doing it.

On marketplace/pricing: these are business model decisions. Naming them on the not list signals you understand the PM/leadership boundary — you're not pretending you can fix everything.

Evidence Slide

→ Slide 04 · "How the Feedback Shaped My Thinking"

Two things to be ready to defend verbally:

  • Why you discounted support quality (Theme 2): Support volume is a symptom of product problems. Fix the platform, ticket volume drops. Investing in headcount without fixing the product treats the symptom. Support will be your biggest ally, not your adversary.
  • Why you discounted pricing complaints (Theme 7): Campspot's pricing is in line with the industry. This is a value demonstration problem for Sales and CS — not a roadmap input you can own.

On the NPS data itself: ~560 written comments from ~3,750 responses. Dissatisfied operators are more motivated to write. The +28 overall NPS is reasonably healthy for B2B software. The written comments skew negative — calibrate accordingly.

On what you discounted from external research: competitor comparison pages (Firefly, ResNexus, Keepr, NewBook) all have inherent bias as vendor-authored content. All pricing and feature claims were cross-referenced against Campspot's own published pages where possible.

The dynamic pricing adoption gap is the best example: operators haven't asked for three-mode control, but that's what would actually fix adoption.
